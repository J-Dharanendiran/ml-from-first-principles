# When One Direction Wasn't Enough

You'd just finished making peace with gradient descent. One parameter, one slope, one clean walk down a curve. It felt settled вҖ” almost too settled.

Then the question that had been waiting quietly in the back of your mind finally spoke up:

> *"What happens when I have more than one thing to optimize at once?"*

Because that's the truth nobody warns you about. Real models don't have one knob. They have two, ten, sometimes billions. And the moment you stop tuning just $m$ and start tuning $m$ *and* $b$ together вҖ” the whole geometry of the problem changes underneath you.

This is where the story stops being about a line on a curve, and starts being about a **landscape**.

---

## 1. The Problem Refuses to Stay Simple

Picture the same dataset you've been living with:

| Hours Studied | Exam Score |
|---|---|
| 2 | 35 |
| 4 | 50 |
| 6 | 65 |
| 8 | 80 |

You want the line

$$y = mx + b$$

Two unknowns. $m$, the slope. $b$, the intercept. And the question underneath the question is always the same one you kept circling back to in the OLS chapter:

> *"Which values of $m$ and $b$ actually give me the best line вҖ” not just a line that works, but the one that minimizes error the most?"*

Every prediction produces an error. You square those errors, average them, and that gives you the **Mean Squared Error**:

$$L(m,b) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(y_i - (mx_i + b))^2$$

Here's the part that changes everything: **the loss isn't a function of one variable anymore.** It's a function of *two*. And a function of two variables doesn't live on a curve. It lives on a **surface**.

---

## 2. The Curve Becomes a Bowl

With one parameter, loss looked like this:

```
Loss
 ^
 |      *
 |    *
 |  *
 |*
 +------------> Weight
```

Simple. One direction to search. But now, with $m$ and $b$ both free to move, the picture becomes three-dimensional:

```
             Loss
               ^
             ____
          __/    \__
        _/          \_
      _/              \_
     /                  \
    ------------------------>
         m          b
```

Every single point on that surface is *one specific model* вҖ” one exact combination of slope and intercept вҖ” and the height at that point is how badly that model performs.

$(m=2, b=5)$ вҶ’ one line вҶ’ one loss.
$(m=4, b=9)$ вҶ’ a different line вҶ’ a different loss.

The entire surface, together, represents *every possible linear model you could ever draw.* Somewhere on it, buried at the lowest point, is the best one.

> *"Why does it always look like a bowl?"*

Because of the square in $(y - (mx+b))^2$. Squaring punishes distance вҖ” the further you drift from the right answer, the faster the error grows, not just linearly but quadratically. That's what carves the smooth, symmetric bowl instead of a jagged, unpredictable terrain. Move away from the minimum in any direction, and the walls rise to meet you.

---

## 3. Starting Blind, on Purpose

Gradient descent never knows the answer in advance. It starts wherever it's told to start вҖ” often somewhere careless, like $m = 4$, $b = -2$ вҖ” and lands *somewhere* on that bowl:

```
          O

       /
     /
   /

Lowest point
```

And immediately it has to answer a question it has no way of knowing yet:

**Which direction is down?**

This is exactly where ordinary differentiation runs out of road.

---

## 4. Why One Derivative Isn't Enough Anymore

Back when you were differentiating $f(x)$, there was only one variable, so there was only one honest answer: $\frac{df}{dx}$.

But $L(m, b)$ has *two* variables baked into it. Move $m$, the loss shifts. Move $b$, the loss shifts differently. So which one do you differentiate?

**Both. Separately. Independently.**

This is the idea of the **partial derivative** вҖ” and it's worth sitting with, because it's the same mechanism that will quietly power every neural network you touch later.

Think about adjusting an office chair. It has a height and a back-angle. If you raise the height and leave the back-angle untouched, that's a *partial* adjustment вҖ” you changed one dimension while freezing the other completely. That's exactly what a partial derivative does to a loss function:

- $\frac{\partial L}{\partial m}$ вҶ’ *"How much does the loss change if I nudge the slope, while holding the intercept perfectly still?"*
- $\frac{\partial L}{\partial b}$ вҶ’ *"How much does the loss change if I nudge the intercept, while holding the slope perfectly still?"*

Two separate questions, asked of the same surface, from the same point.

---

## 5. Working Out the Math вҖ” The Chain Rule Returns

You've met this before. Remember why OLS eventually breaks down at scale, and why gradient descent had to step in? The chain rule was the quiet engine underneath that whole argument, and it comes back here in exactly the same form.

Starting from

$$L = \frac{1}{n}\sum (y - (mx+b))^2$$

the squared term is the giveaway: this is a composition of functions, an *outer* square wrapped around an *inner* expression. The chain rule says: differentiate the outside, multiply by the derivative of the inside.

**With respect to $m$:**

Outer function: $u^2 \rightarrow 2u$
Inner function: $y - (mx + b) \rightarrow$ derivative w.r.t. $m$ is $-x$

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial m} = -\frac{2}{n}\sum x(y - \hat y)$$

**With respect to $b$:**

Same outer derivative, but the inner derivative w.r.t. $b$ is just $-1$:

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial b} = -\frac{2}{n}\sum (y - \hat y)$$

These two lines are not decoration. This is the literal computation that runs, on repeat, inside every gradient descent loop you will ever write.

---

## 6. Reading the Signs

Numbers on their own don't mean anything until you learn to read direction from them.

Suppose $\frac{\partial L}{\partial m} = 20$. Positive. That tells you increasing $m$ increases loss вҖ” so you move **left**, decrease $m$.

Suppose $\frac{\partial L}{\partial m} = -15$. Negative. Increasing $m$ *decreases* loss вҖ” so you move **right**, increase $m$.

Same logic, unchanged, applies to $b$.

---

## 7. Two Derivatives Become One Gradient

Rather than carrying these two numbers around separately, they get bundled into a single object:

$$\nabla L = \begin{bmatrix} \dfrac{\partial L}{\partial m} \\[6pt] \dfrac{\partial L}{\partial b} \end{bmatrix}$$

This is the **gradient** вҖ” and its name is not arbitrary. It points, precisely, in the direction of *steepest increase* in loss. Since the goal is the opposite of increase, the algorithm walks in the reverse direction: $-\nabla L$.

> *"So that's why it's called Gradient **Descent** вҖ” it computes the gradient, then deliberately walks the other way."*

Exactly. The gradient tells you uphill. You do the opposite, on purpose, every single step.

---

## 8. The Update Step

Say the current position is $m = 5$, $b = 3$, and the gradients just computed are $dm = 12$, $db = -4$, with a learning rate $\alpha = 0.01$.

$$m_{new} = 5 - 0.01(12) = 4.88$$
$$b_{new} = 3 - 0.01(-4) = 3.04$$

Notice what just happened: **one parameter went down, the other went up** вҖ” in the same step. Each parameter answers only to its *own* gradient. They move independently, but simultaneously, guided by the same downhill instinct.

More generally, using $\theta$ to represent the whole parameter vector:

$$\theta = \theta - \alpha \nabla L$$

Standing on a hill that rises steeply in one direction, you don't need someone to tell you which way to walk вҖ” you simply walk *away* from the rise. That's the entire intuition compressed into one line of algebra.

---

## 9. The Learning Rate, Revisited

$\alpha$ controls step size, and getting it wrong looks different depending on which way you get it wrong:

**Too small:**
```
.  .  .  .  .
```
Technically correct. Painfully slow.

**Too large:**
```
Jump!! Oops...
Jump!! Oops...
```
Overshoots the minimum, possibly forever, possibly diverging entirely.

**Balanced:**
```
Step вҶ’ Step вҶ’ Step вҶ’ Bottom
```
Fast, stable, convergent.

---

## 10. Why the Loop Has to Repeat

One update was never going to be enough. The moment $m$ and $b$ shift, the *slope of the surface itself* changes beneath them. So the algorithm has no choice but to start over вҖ” recompute the derivatives, rebuild the gradient, take a new step вҖ” again and again:

```
New derivatives вҶ’ New gradient вҶ’ New movement вҶ’ New position
```

This continues until **convergence**, which is declared when any of the following hold:

- The gradient magnitude shrinks below some small threshold вҖ” the surface has gone nearly flat, meaning you're standing at (or very near) the bottom.
- The change in loss between iterations becomes negligible.
- A maximum number of iterations (epochs) is reached, whether or not the bottom was found.

---

## 11. From Two Parameters to Millions

Extend the model slightly:

$$y = w_1x_1 + w_2x_2 + w_3x_3 + b$$

Now there are four parameters instead of two, and the gradient simply grows to match:

$$\nabla L = \begin{bmatrix} \dfrac{\partial L}{\partial w_1} \\[4pt] \dfrac{\partial L}{\partial w_2} \\[4pt] \dfrac{\partial L}{\partial w_3} \\[4pt] \dfrac{\partial L}{\partial b} \end{bmatrix}$$

Nothing about the *method* changes. Only the *size* of the vector changes. Real models can carry millions, even billions, of parameters вҖ” and the loss surface at that point is impossible to draw, let alone imagine. But the mechanism underneath never stops being this same, small idea: compute every partial derivative, stack them into a gradient, step downhill, repeat.

## рҹ”— ML Bridge вҖ” Back to Stroke Prediction

This is not an abstract exercise. In your Stroke Prediction model, you're not tuning one variable вҖ” you're tuning a weight for **age**, a weight for **glucose level**, a weight for **BMI**, a weight for **hypertension status**, and more, all simultaneously, all sitting inside one high-dimensional loss surface you'll never be able to sketch on paper.

Multi-variable gradient descent is *the exact mechanism* that walks that surface downhill for you вҖ” computing a partial derivative for every single feature's weight, bundling them into one gradient vector, and updating all of them together, on every epoch, until the model converges on the combination of weights that best separates stroke risk from non-risk. The math you just worked through by hand with $m$ and $b$ is the same math running silently underneath every training step of that model.

---

## рҹ§© Deepest Realization

The jump from one-variable to multi-variable gradient descent isn't really a jump in *difficulty* вҖ” it's a jump in *dimension*. The bowl becomes a surface, the surface becomes a hypersurface no one can visualize, and yet the walking instructions never change: find the slope in every direction you're allowed to move, and step away from the ones that go up.

Partial differentiation is what makes that possible вҖ” the discipline of asking *"what if I changed only this one thing, and froze everything else?"*, over and over, once per parameter, until you have a complete picture of the terrain in every direction at once.

That's the whole trick. Not new math. Just the same old derivative, asked politely, one variable at a time.

---

> *"Gradient Descent didn't get smarter as the problem got bigger вҖ” it just kept asking the same small question, once for every parameter it was given, and trusted that enough small honest answers would eventually add up to the right direction down."*
